# Initial Foundations: The Jain Game of Self-Knowledge

## The Primordial Vision
The initial version of Gyan Chaupar was strictly rooted in **Jain Cosmography and Ontological Ethics**. It was designed not merely as a game, but as a pedagogical tool for a Seeker (*Śrāvaka*) to understand the intricate paths of **Karma (Action)** and **Nirjara (Shedding of Karma)**.

---

## 🏛️ The Structure of the Cosmos (The Board)
Unlike modern variations, the initial Jain board was often structured with **84 Squares**:
- **The Number 84**: Representative of the *8.4 million cycles of rebirth* (yoni) that a soul must traverse through the realms of existence.
- **The Three Worlds (Triloka)**:
    - **Adho Loka (Lower World)**: The bottom rows, filled with snakes representing the hellish states (*Naraka*) and heavy base instincts.
    - **Madhya Loka (Middle World)**: The central rows, representing the human realm (*Manushya Loka*) where the soul has the agency to choose between virtue and vice.
    - **Urdhva Loka (Upper World)**: The top rows, representing the celestial realms (*Deva Loka*) and the abode of the Tirthankaras.
- **The Goal: Siddhashila**: The final destination is not just a high number, but the **Siddhashila**—a crescent-shaped realm at the very top of the cosmos where liberated souls (*Siddhas*) reside in eternal bliss.

---

## 🐍 Serpentine Vices (The Four Kashayas)
In the initial Jain versions, the snakes were personifications of the **Four Kashayas (Passions)** that bind the soul to the cycle of rebirth:
1. **Krodha (Anger)**: Dragging the soul down from states of calm to agitation.
2. **Maana (Pride/Ego)**: Blinding the soul to its true, humble nature.
3. **Maya (Deceit)**: Tangling the spirit in a web of illusions.
4. **Lobha (Greed)**: The infinite desire that leads to a heavy fall back to the lower worlds.

Other snakes represented **Mithyatva (False Belief)**—the most dangerous state that blocks the path to liberation.

---

## 🪜 Spiritual Ladders (The Mahavratas)
The ladders were defined by the **Five Great Vows (Mahavratas)** and various spiritual virtues that provide "speed" on the path to Moksha:
1. **Ahimsa (Non-violence)**: The primary ladder, providing the swiftest ascent.
2. **Satya (Truth)**: A steady path to the upper worlds.
3. **Asteya (Non-stealing)**: Liberation from material craving.
4. **Brahmacharya (Chastity)**: The preservation of spiritual energy.
5. **Aparigraha (Non-attachment)**: The lightening of the soul so it may float upward.

---

## 🎨 Historical Artistry
The initial version’s aesthetic was governed by **Jain Miniature Painting (Pattachitra)**:
- **Colors**: Derived from semi-precious stones and minerals (Lapis Lazuli, Cinnabar, Orpiment).
- **Styling**: Large, wide-angled eyes on all figures, sharp anatomical lines, and dense, floral marginalia.
- **Iconography**: The board itself often took the shape of the **Lokapurusha (The Cosmic Man)**, where the game cells were mapped directly to the anatomy of the universe.

---

## 📜 Pedagogical Objective
The goal of the player was to witness **Samyak Darshan (Right Faith)** through the game’s randomness, understanding that while the dice of life are unpredictable, following the **Ratnatraya (The Three Jewels)**—Right Faith, Right Knowledge, and Right Conduct—is the only way to eventually bypass the snakes and reach the final sanctuary.
